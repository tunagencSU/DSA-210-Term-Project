# Project Title: Spatial and Temporal Analysis of Camping Sites in the Black Sea Region: A Decision Support Model

# 1. Motivation and Objective

The objective of this project is to conduct a comprehensive spatial and temporal analysis of 30 prominent camping and highland locations in the Black Sea Region. For a balanced modeling approach, these locations are evenly distributed across the Eastern (10), Central (10), and Western (10) Black Sea sub-regions. The primary goal is to build a predictive machine learning model that integrates dynamic variables such as transportation infrastructure, meteorological conditions, terrain status, human mobility, and the probability of encountering wildlife. The developed model aims to present the human density and weather conditions for the desired months, along with the overall risk table generated by calculating these variables.


# 2. Data Sources and Collection Methodology

There is no centralized dataset containing all the specific parameters for these 30 locations. Therefore, the "Unified Risk Dataset" will be constructed by collecting and cleaning data obtained from independent sources through the following pipeline:

* **Meteorological Data:** Historical weather time series (TMAX, TMIN, total precipitation) will be extracted at a weekly resolution using the **meteostat** Python library, utilizing spatial interpolation algorithms for high-altitude areas without direct measurement stations.
* **Popularity and Digital Footprint Proxy:** Official visitor statistics are not available for isolated locations. This data gap will be overcome by applying web scraping techniques to outdoor sports platforms like Wikiloc and general Google comments to the stated camp sites.
* **Isolation and Healthcare Access:** To accurately measure spatial isolation and avoid the misleading nature of bird's-eye (Euclidean) distances, actual driving times to the nearest hospitals will be calculated using the OSMnx library, OpenSourceRouting, GraphHopper, and OpenStreetMap (OSM) routing algorithms.
* **Terrain and Soil Risk (Data Enrichment):** NASA SRTM Digital Elevation Models (DEM) will provide slope data, while the SoilGrids REST API will offer soil composition (clay/sand ratios). By combining this data with OSM road surface tags, the risks of mudslides and road closures that may occur during heavy precipitation will be modeled on a weekly basis.
* **Wildlife Encounter Risk (HWC):** A synthetic risk score will be developed based on current empirical spatial ecology research regarding human-brown bear conflicts (e.g., Şekercioğlu et al., 2024). This score will utilize Human Footprint Index (HFI) raster data, regional base risk coefficients, and distance algorithms calculating proximity to villages, and will be dynamically weighted according to the biological season of the wildlife (e.g., hyperphagia or hibernation weeks).

# 3. Data Characteristics

Initially, five years of weather data will be acquired using Meteostat,. The meteorological conditions in the regions will be tabulated at weekly intervals. Subsequently, human activity in these areas will be examined using data extracted from Google Maps reviews and Wikiloc posts to analyze the weeks with higher human density. Finally, the soil type, transportation infrastructure, and wildlife population of the region will be assessed, and a machine learning model will be developed using these datasets. The resulting model will predict the weather conditions and population density for any given date, while simultaneously conducting a general risk analysis.


# 4. Research Questions and Sub-Questions

### Main Question
How does the seasonal popularity and human density of camping sites in the Black Sea Region change through the interaction of dynamic meteorological conditions and static spatial factors (infrastructure, soil composition, wildlife risk)?

### Sub-Questions
* How do dynamic meteorological variables, such as precipitation and temperature, impact the weekly human mobility of camping sites (measured via Wikiloc route usage and Google Maps interactions)?
* How does the spatial isolation of a camping site (driving distance to a hospital and road surface quality) shape campers' location preferences and the overall annual human density in the area?
* Does the soil composition (clay/sand ratio) of a camping site exacerbate the negative impact of adverse weather conditions (e.g., heavy precipitation) on site popularity by increasing the risk of mudslides and road closures?

# 5. Hypotheses

* **H1:** More favorable meteorological conditions (lower total precipitation and optimum air temperature) are associated with increased human mobility and popularity at camping sites.
* **H2:** Camping sites with high spatial accessibility (short driving distance to a hospital and asphalt road infrastructure) have a significantly higher annual human density compared to isolated and hard-to-reach areas.
* **H3:** The decline in popularity during periods of heavy precipitation varies across locations: camping sites with a high clay ratio in their soil composition (carrying a higher risk of mud/landslides) experience a statistically more severe drop in visitors compared to areas with sandy or rocky compositions.
* **H0 (Null):** There is no statistically significant relationship between the meteorological conditions, spatial isolation levels, and soil characteristics of camping sites and the human mobility (popularity index) within these areas.

# 6. Methodology
# 6.1 Data Structuring and Index Construction

Two primary datasets are constructed to form the decision support model:
* **Static Spatial Dataset:** Contains fixed geographical and infrastructural attributes for all 30 locations, including driving time to the nearest hospital, road surface type, and soil composition (clay/sand ratio).
* **Dynamic 52-Week Panel Dataset:** A time-series dataset merging the 5-year averaged weekly meteorological conditions (temperature, precipitation) with the composite human footprint/popularity index (derived from Wikiloc and Google interactions).

Standardized composite scores are constructed for the decision-support model, such as:
* **Environmental Risk Score** = $z$(precipitation) + $z$(clay ratio)
* **Accessibility Score** = $z$(hospital proximity) + $z$(asphalt/road quality)

# 6.2 Exploratory Data Analysis

Exploratory analysis visualizes seasonal mobility trends across 52 weeks, spatial differences among the Eastern, Central, and Western Black Sea sub-regions, and the baseline relationships between weather conditions, accessibility, and camping site popularity.

# 6.3 Hypothesis Testing

Statistical inference is used to validate the relationships between the raw features before training the predictive models:
* **H1:** Pearson or Spearman correlation tests evaluating the linear/monotonic relationship between weekly weather variables (temperature/precipitation) and the weekly popularity index.
* **H2:** Independent samples t-tests (or Mann-Whitney U tests) comparing the annual human density of high-accessibility sites versus low-accessibility (isolated) sites.
* **H3:** Two-way ANOVA or multiple linear regression with an interaction term:
  * Popularity Decline ~ Precipitation × Clay Ratio (to measure how soil type exacerbates the impact of rain on visitor numbers).

# 6.4 Machine Learning Analysis

Machine learning methods are used to complement statistical inference by building the predictive decision-support system:
* **Regression Models:** Linear Regression, Ridge, Lasso, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) are used to dynamically predict the exact Human Density / Popularity Index for any given location and week.
* **Classification/Clustering Task:** K-Means clustering or classification models to group locations into distinct "Camper Profiles" (e.g., High-Risk/Isolated, Safe/Accessible) based on predicted weather and spatial data.
* **Evaluation:** K-Fold cross-validation and time-aware splits; performance evaluated using RMSE and R² for regression, and confusion matrices for classification.

# 7. Expected Results

The project expects to find a strong positive correlation between favorable meteorological conditions (optimal temperature, low precipitation) and increased camping site popularity. Furthermore, spatial accessibility is anticipated to be a primary driver of baseline annual human density, with easily accessible sites attracting significantly more visitors. A key finding is expected to demonstrate that soil composition significantly moderates visitor decline during adverse weather; specifically, clay-heavy areas are expected to experience a much sharper drop in popularity during heavy precipitation due to mud and road closure risks. Ultimately, the predictive model is expected to successfully categorize these 30 locations into distinct, dynamically changing "Camper Profiles," serving as a robust decision-support tool.
